What this page is. This is Harbour straight from QuickBooks — no projections, just what actually happened. The big lesson up top: a single quarter tells you almost nothing about this business. Because you bill at project milestones but pay for labor and materials as the work happens, one quarter can show a 74% margin and the next can show a loss — same healthy company, just different timing. Always read the trailing-twelve-month (TTM) and full-year numbers, never a single quarter in isolation.

Read this first. Harbour's monthly gross margin in QuickBooks swings from +80% to −1,000% because revenue is booked at project milestones while job costs land as incurred. Single months are noise. FY2025 looked like 52.8% GM (revenue ahead of cost); 2026-YTD looks like 6.7% (cost ahead of revenue). The honest, blended trailing-twelve-month gross margin is 33.3% — that's where this model starts. The single biggest lever on your exit value is moving that number toward 40%. Buyer-normalized basis: this model values Harbour the way an arms-length buyer would — it subtracts a $180K owner replacement salary (the cost to hire management) and assumes no owner distributions, so the EBITDA shown is what the business earns after paying for someone to run it.

How the exit value is built

The whole model in three plain steps. The numbers fill in from your dials.
1
Find the real profit. Start with sales, take out job costs (labor + materials) and overhead, then subtract a $180K salary to hire someone to run it without you. What's left is Normalized EBITDA — the honest yearly profit a buyer cares about. —
2
Put a price on that profit. Buyers don't pay for one year — they pay a multiple of it. At 4.0×, the business itself is worth the equity value (the price for the company). —
3
Add what's already yours. On top of the sale price you keep the cash in the bank at closing, plus any draws you took along the way. Together that's your total take-home. —

Why "gross margin" is the dial that matters most: every extra point of margin is profit that costs you nothing more to produce — and then it gets multiplied at sale. Moving from 33% to 40% margin doesn't just add profit; it adds that profit 4× over in the final price.
