Purpose

The cutover is the single most consequential part of onboarding. Everything else can be fixed later; a books takeover that starts from opening balances that never tied is a wound that reopens at every close for a year, and again at tax time. The whole job of this SOP is to make the first period Turnkey owns start from a clean, verified, agreed-upon baseline, with no month dropped between the prior bookkeeper's last close and ours, and no month accidentally posted twice.

This SOP is invoked by the go-live runbook at its cutover step and runs in parallel with the rest of onboarding. It covers the conversation with the outgoing bookkeeper, the exact records to pull, how to prove the opening balances are right, where the cutover date gets recorded so automation respects it, and the payroll parallel run that keeps year-end forms correct. The goal is a signed-off opening baseline the first close can build on without doubt.

Owner and roles

Role Who Responsibility in cutover
Prior-firm relationship, escalation, terms Ricky Contacts the outgoing bookkeeper or CPA directly when the handoff stalls; owns anything touching fees or scope.
Cutover execution and QC Accounting Manager Runs the handoff call, collects the records, drives the tie-out, sets the cutover date, verifies the parallel run.
Opening-balance tie-out Bookkeeper (Hardik) Ties opening balances to the beginning trial balance, reconciles each account to its last-reconciled date, confirms nothing double-counts.
Client side Named primary contact Authorizes the release of records and access from the outgoing firm.

Cadence and triggers

  • Trigger: the engagement letter is signed and the go-live runbook reaches the cutover step. Records requests can begin as soon as the client authorizes the outgoing firm to release them.
  • Handoff call: schedule the outgoing-bookkeeper handoff as early as possible; a live conversation surfaces quirks that a records dump never will.
  • The cutover date is the hardest date in onboarding when payroll is involved. Treat any slippage past roughly two weeks as a blown transition to be reset, not absorbed.
  • No coverage gap. The outgoing firm locks the last period before cutover; a brief intentional overlap is fine, a gap is not.
  • Done means tied. The cutover is not complete when the records arrive; it is complete when the opening balances tie, the cutover date is recorded in both systems, and (if applicable) the payroll parallel run has matched.

The process

Step 1: The prior-bookkeeper handoff call

Run one structured call (or a tight thread if a call is impossible) with the outgoing bookkeeper or CPA, with the client's authorization in hand. Treat them the way this SOP asks outgoing firms to treat us: professional, specific, no surprises. Agenda:

Step 2: Records request list

Request these from the outgoing firm or the client, in one clear list, with a secure channel named for anything carrying PII. Track each item until it arrives.

Step 3: Opening-balance verification (do not skip)

This is where a takeover is won or lost. Work every account; never force a tie.

Step 4: Set the cutover date in both systems

The cutover date has to live in two places so that both people and automation respect it.

Step 5: Payroll parallel run (any client where we take over payroll)

Payroll is the least forgiving handoff, because a mistake surfaces as a wrong W-2 that cannot be fixed with an apology.

Step 6: Sign off the baseline

Common failure modes

These are patterns seen across takeovers, written as patterns rather than as any one client's story. Watch for each.

  • The three-way rent (or any recurring expense) reconciled inconsistently. A single recurring payment coded to different accounts across the prior periods, or split and recombined differently month to month, so it never nets cleanly at cutover. Trace the recurring items specifically before trusting the trial balance; do not assume a clean summary means clean detail.
  • Credentials lost mid-flight. Access that existed during the handoff call is gone by the time the tie-out needs it, because the outgoing person left or a shared login was changed. Capture and vault every needed credential at first contact, per the Credential Intake and Vaulting SOP; never rely on a login staying available.
  • Opening balances that will not tie to the last return. Usually a prior-period adjustment or a reclass the outgoing firm made after the last financials we were given. Get the adjusting entries, do not plug the difference.
  • Uncleared items double-counted at first reconciliation. The first close re-clears a transaction the prior firm already cleared, or drops one still outstanding, because the last-reconciled date and uncleared list were not captured. This is why Step 3 captures them explicitly.
  • Payroll year-to-date mismatch caught after the first run. The single most damaging pattern, because it produces wrong W-2s. The parallel-run verification in Step 5 exists solely to catch it before a live run.
  • A coverage gap between the two firms. Neither firm owns the boundary month, so it silently drops. The handoff call fixes the exact last period the prior firm locks, so there is overlap, never a gap.

Guardrails

  • Never skip opening-balance verification. Shortcutting the tie-out is the number one way months get dropped or duplicated.
  • Never force a tie. A difference that will not resolve is a real error to find, not a number to plug. Stop and escalate.
  • Never skip the payroll parallel run. Wrong W-2s are not recoverable with an apology.
  • books_closed_date is set in both QBO and Supabase before automation runs. An empty value silently defaults to full manual review and can let write-back reach a protected period.
  • No books-of-record work before the letter is signed and the baseline is agreed. Research and access collection can start earlier; posting cannot.
  • PII travels only through a named secure channel. Pay registers, I-9s, and returns never sit in open email threads or the checklist longer than needed.
  • Turnkey CFO is a bookkeeping firm. Cutover language stays in bookkeeping terms; tax positions and return questions route to the client's CPA or attorney.

Escalation

Situation Action
Opening balances will not tie to the prior close or the last return Stop before posting anything; escalate to the Bookkeeper then Ricky. Do not force the tie.
Outgoing bookkeeper is unresponsive or withholding records Escalate to Ricky with specifics; he decides the client-side approach and contacts the client directly.
Payroll handoff is at risk of missing the cutover date Flag to Ricky immediately; he contacts the client. Reset the transition date rather than run on unverified figures.
Payroll year-to-date figures do not match in the parallel run Do not run the first live cycle; escalate and resolve the mismatch first.
A needed credential is lost during the handoff Follow the Credential Intake and Vaulting SOP; escalate to Ricky if access cannot be restored.
A tax or return question arises during tie-out Redirect to the client's CPA or attorney; do not interpret a tax position.
