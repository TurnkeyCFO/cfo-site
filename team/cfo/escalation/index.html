Purpose

This SOP defines what truly requires Ricky's attention and what the team owns end to end. The single most valuable habit on this team is knowing the difference. Ricky's biggest operational risk is not client acquisition, it is attention overload: every item routed to him that the team could have handled makes the whole firm slower.

The bias, always: surface what truly needs Ricky, delegate or handle everything else. When in doubt, run it up one rung of the ladder (not straight to the top) and let that person decide.

Owner and roles

Beth Accounting Manager, QC layer above Hardik (California-based) Slack plus the Outlook role address First-pass review of Hardik's work, client-facing communication, the close calendar, Hardik's daily priorities

Two structural notes:

  • Hardik and Beth's time zones (India plus Pacific) create near-24-hour coverage on purpose. Hand work across the overlap rather than holding it.
  • During Beth's ramp, Ricky co-reviews her client-facing drafts before send. At full release that review step drops and Beth communicates with clients directly, with Ricky as backup.

Cadence and triggers

Standing rhythms that keep escalation from ever being a surprise:

  • Hardik and Ricky hold a weekly ops sync every Monday. Systemic issues (capacity, recurring quality patterns, a client whose books keep breaking the same way) belong there, not in ad hoc pings.
  • The monthly close (BD1, BD3, BD6, BD10, BD12) has built-in escalation checkpoints. A client who has sent nothing by BD3 is escalated internally the same day. A month that is materially incomplete at BD6 is escalated before delivery, never shipped quietly.
  • Client emails are acknowledged the same business day, even if the full answer comes later ("received, working on it, you will have it by X").
  • Internal Slack messages get a reply within a couple of working hours.
  • If a promised deliverable date is at risk, the client hears from Turnkey before the date, not after.

Immediate triggers (do not batch, do not wait for a sync):

  • A client is frustrated, upset, or has gone quiet. Flag to Ricky the same day with your read on why.
  • Anything in the books that looks wrong: large unusual withdrawals, possible personal transactions run through the business, missing deposits, revenue inconsistencies, negative cash trends. Escalate immediately.
  • A missing login or access gap. Post it in Slack the day you find it. Never work around a missing login or borrow someone else's credentials.

The process

The escalation ladder

The ladder runs bookkeeper to Accounting Manager to Ricky. Each rung resolves what it can and passes up only what it cannot.

Situation Path

Client asks about their books, timing, or a specific transaction The AM handles it directly (Ricky reviews drafts during Beth's ramp)
Anything touching pricing, scope, or engagement terms Ricky, always. Never negotiate or promise scope on your own
Tax or legal advice request Do not answer. Redirect the client to "your CPA or attorney" and send a holding reply
Client frustrated, upset, or gone quiet Ricky, same day, with context

Hardik lanes and SLA expectations

Hardik works in Financial Cents, not Slack. Route QBO work, transaction questions, and book cleanup to him there.

Hardik runs his own independent practice. He is a trusted long-term partner, not an employee, so priorities are set through the Accounting Manager and the weekly sync rather than assumed.

Beth QC layer

Beth sits above Hardik as the first-pass reviewer and the client-facing quality gate. Nothing Hardik produces goes to a client without passing through her review.

Beth's first-pass review does not yet have a written checklist; it is run on judgment while she ramps (confirm with Ricky). When that checklist is defined it will live in the Accounting Manager role SOP.

The NEEDS-RICKY protocol

When something genuinely needs Ricky, flag it so he can act on it in seconds.

Topics that always trigger this protocol, no interpretation, no advisory answer:

  • Financial-analysis questions ("why is my margin down?")
  • Cash-flow concerns
  • Budget strategy
  • Tax questions (redirect to their CPA or attorney, then flag)
  • Pricing, discounts, or anything about the fee
  • Strategic recommendations
  • Emotional or relationship-sensitive matters

What Ricky always holds

Some things are never delegated, no matter how capable the team gets. These are the client-relationship anchors:

  • Pricing, scope, and engagement terms, including renewals, expansions, and anything a client could hear as a promise
  • New engagement decisions and proposals
  • Frustrated or at-risk client relationships (the team flags, Ricky owns the save)
  • Strategic and advisory conversations
  • Board-level and senior-pastor presentations, and any premium review meeting
  • Contractor and team decisions (rates, roles, hiring, discipline)

Everything else is presumed delegatable. If a task does not touch one of these anchors, the default answer is: the team handles it.

Guardrails

  • Never negotiate pricing, scope, or engagement terms with a client. Not even a hint, not even "I am sure that would be fine."
  • Never answer a tax or legal question. The line is always "your CPA or attorney."
  • Never deliver financials you would not stand behind. Materially incomplete months get escalated before BD6, not shipped with fingers crossed.
  • Never let failure language reach a client. If a deliverable is broken or late, the client hears a plain, calm status from us before the deadline, and never an error message or an excuse dump.
  • Never work around a missing login or borrow credentials. Surface the gap the day you find it.
  • Never mix internal commentary into a client-visible surface (Client Chat in Financial Cents, client email threads).
  • Never send client-facing email from a personal address. Role addresses only.
  • Escalating too little is worse than escalating too much, but the fix for over-escalating is to escalate to the right rung, not to stop escalating.

Escalation

This SOP is itself the escalation reference, so the meta-rules are short:

  • If you are unsure whether something needs Ricky, ask the Accounting Manager first. That question is exactly what the middle rung is for.
  • If the Accounting Manager is unavailable and the item is same-day urgent (upset client, money risk, blown deadline), go straight to Ricky in Slack with context. Skipping a rung for genuine urgency is correct, not rude.
  • If Ricky is unreachable and a client is waiting, send the holding acknowledgment ("received, we are on it, you will hear from us by X") and keep the flag active in Slack until he responds.
  • If this SOP conflicts with something Ricky has said directly, his direction wins. Flag the conflict so the SOP gets updated.
