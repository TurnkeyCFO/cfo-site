Purpose

This SOP defines the productized Turnkey CFO proposal: the repeatable version of the gold-standard proposal that already wins. The core idea is that the proposal is not a document that argues a price. It is a diagnosis built on the prospect's own real numbers, wrapped in a relationship-framed briefing that names the stakes and reframes the decision away from cost.

Two artifacts, not one: a diagnostic dashboard (the proof, built live on the prospect's data) and a briefing page (the pitch, one click from the dashboard). Pricing lives outside both artifacts, delivered as a deliberate follow-up. This SOP documents each artifact section by section, the hosting rule, a lighter one-pager variant for smaller deals, the canonical tier names, and the single rule that governs every number: it comes from the pricing ladder.

Turnkey CFO is a bookkeeping firm. Proposal language is bookkeeping, payroll, 1099, AP/AR, monthly close, and reporting. The banned "fractional" phrase never appears; the top tier is called Reporting & Advisory.

Cadence and triggers

  • Trigger: a discovery call ends with the assessment-or-demo next step (see the Discovery Call Runbook). The proposal is the deliverable that next step points to.
  • Prerequisite: read-only access to the prospect's real data (QBO, plus any giving or payment platform). The whole model is "built on your real numbers," so the assessment access is what unlocks the proposal.
  • Sequence: access granted, dashboard built on live data, briefing page written around it, both hosted on the brand domain (noindex), link sent. Pricing follows as a separate tiered quote within one business day, never printed inside the proposal.
  • Smaller deals: where a full two-artifact build is not warranted (a simple single-entity SMB), use the light one-pager variant below instead.

The process

Step 1: The two-artifact anatomy

Artifact A, the diagnostic dashboard (the proof). A live, read-only view built on the prospect's actual QBO data, organized by altitude so a non-financial decision-maker can read it:

  • A layered structure from the highest level down to full detail. The proven shape is roughly: a top-level health view (plain-language verdict plus the multi-year arc), a trends and history view, a ministries-or-segments view, a budget-versus-actual view, a giving or revenue deep-dive, and a full-detail view.
  • The top-level view leads with a plain-language health card above the numbers, not a wall of figures. Say where things stand in a sentence, then show the chart.
  • A visible provenance line proving it runs on their real data ("Source: QuickBooks Online, live," through a stated month). The provenance is the credibility: it is manifestly their numbers, not a template.

Artifact B, the relationship briefing page (the pitch). One click from the dashboard, this is the actual proposal. It frames the relationship, names the stakes, restates the dashboard as a service feature, defuses the transition fear by name, and reframes the decision away from price.

Step 2: The briefing page, section by section

Build the briefing in this order. This is the documented anatomy of the pattern that works.

  1. Title and hook. A short, forward-looking line about clarity for their next chapter.
  2. Relationship framing, not transactional. Position Turnkey as a partner who already understands their world and their people, not a vendor bolting on a service.
  3. Name the stakes. What they are outgrowing (reporting they cannot read, a provider who cannot sit in the room) set against what is at stake for them (a campaign, a growth phase, a board that needs clean numbers).
  4. "Where things stand today, in plain language." Live health cards translated into plain terms, with any honest reframing (a deliberate investment-year position, a trend turning) stated straight. Numbers here are the prospect's own, shown in the live dashboard, not printed as fees.
  5. "What you will actually see." Restate the dashboard's layered structure as a service feature, and embed a live button straight into the working dashboard.
  6. The full scope of partnership. A set of service tiles covering the offer: core bookkeeping, readable reporting, budget versus actual, being "in the room" at their meeting cadence, campaign or program reporting where relevant, giving or revenue insight, automation, cleanup, and a real partner relationship.
  7. Transition-risk defusal, by name. Name the specific fear ("we know the last switch took a long time; that will not happen here") and follow it with a concrete commitment (first clean monthly report within weeks, the transition handled on our side).
  8. A numbered path across a real calendar. Week one, weeks two to three, month two, and ongoing, each with a plain outcome. A simple path removes the fear of the unknown.
  9. Structure section where relevant. For a campaign or a multi-phase program, break it into named phases so the client sees we understand the shape of what they are doing.
  10. Closing reframe. End by moving the decision off price and onto clarity and partnership.
  11. Footer methodology. Disclose the data source plainly (live QBO, read-only, through a stated month).

Pricing lives outside both artifacts. No fee, tier price, or dollar figure is printed inside the dashboard or the briefing. Pricing is the deliberate follow-up quote. This is deliberate: the proposal sells clarity and partnership, and the price conversation happens on its own, sourced from the ladder.

Step 3: Hosting

  • Host both artifacts on the brand domain, never on an internal HUD host and never on a bare preview URL. Per the deliverable-hosting rule, a client-facing page is served from turnkeycfo.com (or the relevant brand apex), added as a subdirectory of the brand's site repo so it deploys on push.
  • Mark the proposal pages noindex so they do not surface in search.
  • Send the client the branded link, not the build URL.

Step 4: The light one-pager variant (smaller deals)

For a simple single-entity SMB where a full two-artifact build is not warranted, use a single briefing one-pager instead of the dashboard-plus-briefing pair:

  • The gap named plainly, the outcome we deliver, the tier that fits, the simple path (take the keys, first clean report, ongoing rhythm), and the reframe off price.
  • Still no printed fee: pricing follows as the tiered quote, or, only where the discovery runbook's live-pricing exception applies (a simple single-entity SMB meeting every part of the ladder's simple-SMB gate), an on-ladder range confirmed in writing.
  • Still hosted on the brand domain (noindex) or delivered as a branded PDF, never off-brand.

Step 5: Tier names and the pricing rule

  • Canonical tier names, always: Books / Books + Reporting (the recommended lead tier) / Reporting & Advisory. Use these exact names in every proposal and one-pager. Never the banned "fractional" phrase.
  • Every number comes from the ladder. Any figure in any quote, one-pager, or letter traces to the pricing ladder (pricing-model/pricing.json, internal). No hardcoded fee that is not on the ladder, and no bespoke off-ladder number. Relationship or referral adjustments stay on-ladder through the ladder's sanctioned override construct, with a reason recorded, never an invented one-off price.

Guardrails

  • The proposal is a diagnosis, not a price argument. Lead with the dashboard built on their real numbers; the briefing frames it. Never open with a fee.
  • No fees or dollar figures inside the dashboard or the briefing. Pricing is the separate follow-up quote, sourced from the ladder.
  • Every number traces to the pricing ladder. No hardcoded or off-ladder fee anywhere in the package.
  • Canonical tier names only. Books / Books + Reporting / Reporting & Advisory. Never the banned "fractional" phrase; the top tier is Reporting & Advisory.
  • Brand-domain hosting only. Client-facing proposals live on the brand apex (noindex), never on an internal HUD host or a bare preview link. Send the branded link.
  • The prospect's own data, read-only. The dashboard runs on their live numbers through a stated month, view-only. Never move money, never write back.
  • No failure language, ever. Nothing broken or degraded reaches a prospect. If a dashboard is not live and clean, hold the send and fix it first.
  • The client's own brand on client-facing assets. Where a proposal carries the client's masthead or accent, use their real brand, never a generic template palette or another client's.

Escalation

Situation Action
Prospect asks for a printed price inside the proposal Hold; pricing is the follow-up quote from the ladder. Bring the shape to Ricky.
A needed number is not on the pricing ladder Ricky, before it goes in any artifact. Never invent an off-ladder figure.
Read-only data access cannot be obtained The dashboard cannot be built on real data; escalate to Ricky to decide the fallback (a one-pager, or wait for access).
A relationship or referral adjustment is requested Ricky, via the ladder's sanctioned override with a recorded reason; never a silent one-off price.
Anything touching scope, terms, or "fractional" language slipping in Ricky, always.
