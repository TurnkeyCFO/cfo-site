Purpose

This SOP covers everything between "a prospect books a call" and "the deal is either qualified with a next step, or cleanly disqualified." A good discovery call does three things: it proves we already understand the prospect's world, it surfaces the whole operational picture in one sitting (so nothing shows up as a surprise after signing), and it ends with a real next step (a free assessment, a live-data demo, or a scoped quote) instead of a price argued in the room.

The pattern this runbook encodes is the one that already works: show up having looked, name the specific provable gap, diagnose before pricing, and let the client's real numbers be the pitch. The failure this runbook designs against is the opposite: scope that leaks in reactively after signing, a crisis deadline that shortcuts diligence, and promises made in the room that never get written down.

Turnkey CFO is a bookkeeping firm. Our language on every call is bookkeeping, payroll, 1099, AP/AR, monthly close, and reporting. Tax and legal questions get redirected to "your CPA or attorney," always.

Cadence and triggers

  • Trigger: a prospect books an intro call (Calendly) or replies to a thread asking to talk. The booking is the forcing function for pre-call prep: no call opens without the prep pull done.
  • Pre-call prep window: the ten minutes before the call. If prep cannot be finished in time, the call still runs, but the lead opens by validating the intake data rather than assuming it.
  • Diagnosis before price: the default is a free assessment or a live-data demo built on the prospect's real numbers, then pricing as a deliberate follow-up. A live number is the exception, not the norm (see the hard rules).
  • 24-hour rule: any pricing that is not given live goes out as a tiered quote within one business day. Any open question raised on the call gets an explicit "I will follow up by (date)" owner and date, in the follow-up note.
  • Decision-gate respect: if the prospect names a real gate (a board vote, a chairman weighing another hire, a fiscal-year timing), the lead honors the window instead of pushing past it. Set a follow-up date to the gate, not before it.

The process

Everything a submitted-estimate prospect has already told us lives in the intake data. The call refines that picture; it does not re-derive it from scratch.

Step 1: Pre-call prep (pull before the call)

Pull and skim these before the call opens. The goal is to arrive asking sharper questions, not generic ones.

Step 2: The call arc

Run the call in this order. It is a diagnosis conversation, not a demo of features and not a price negotiation.

  1. Researched opening. Reference the specific thing they shared when booking, or the number they self-selected on the estimate. Open having looked. This is the first credibility signal.
  2. Name the specific, provable gap. Restate their actual pain back to them in their own domain terms (restricted fund tracking and audit compliance for a nonprofit, budget-vs-actual that missed a board meeting for a church, books months behind for an SMB). Naming it precisely proves we already understand their world. When a concrete incumbent failure exists and the prospect already knows it, surface it plainly. Never manufacture one.
  3. Diagnosis conversation. Pull the full operational picture out in this one sitting (the scoping checklist in Step 3 is the backbone). Ask about the whole stack, not just the books. Write down every open question you cannot resolve on the spot rather than guessing at it.
  4. Offer the free assessment or live-data demo. The assessment is the pitch. Ask for read access (QBO plus any giving or payment platform), then build a demo dashboard on their real numbers for their board, owner, or chairman. "A working sample built on your real numbers" beats any deck.
  5. Tier-fit discussion (direction, not a printed price). Talk through which tier fits what you just heard (Books / Books + Reporting / Reporting & Advisory), and what would move them up or down a tier. Keep it about fit and outcome, not a number, unless the live-pricing exception in the hard rules applies.
  6. Next-step close. End with one concrete next step and a date: access granted for the assessment, the demo build scheduled, or the tiered quote promised within one business day. Reframe the decision away from price ("the question is clarity and partnership, not the cheapest line item"). Offer a real reference (a similar-size, similar-type client) when it helps, with that client's permission.

Step 3: Scoping checklist (the full-stack inventory)

Work this in the diagnosis conversation. The point is to surface everything now, so nothing accumulates reactively after signing. Drop what does not apply, but start from the full list so nothing is re-derived later.

Step 4: Qualification: good-fit and bad-fit signals

Read these against the client type. A good-fit call ends with an assessment or quote; a bad-fit call ends with a kind, clear "we may not be the right fit," which is a win too.

Client type Good-fit signals Bad-fit signals
Church Wants readable reporting and a partner "in the room," values stewardship insight, has a real fund structure to steward, decision-maker is engaged Wants only the cheapest possible data entry, no interest in reporting or cadence, unwilling to grant view access
Nonprofit (non-church) Grant and restricted-fund tracking needs, board or finance-committee reporting cadence, audit or 990 pressure Expects us to be the auditor or the CPA, wants tax filing we do not do, no clear reporting owner
Single-entity SMB Books behind and wants them current, clear on who signs, values on-time monthly financials Chasing a one-time cleanup with no ongoing intent, price-only shopper, no access willingness
Growth SMB Multiple systems and real transaction volume, wants reporting to make decisions, ready to invest in getting current Scope keeps expanding verbally with no willingness to define it, wants advisory we scope as reporting
Multi-entity Intercompany or multi-location complexity they already feel, wants one clean consolidated view Structure is undefined and they want us to design the legal entity structure (that is their CPA or attorney)

Cross-type bad-fit signals (any client): unwilling to grant view-only access, wants tax or legal advice as the core service, treats price as the only variable, or a decision-maker who will not engage. Flag any of these to Ricky rather than forcing the deal.

Step 5: The 24-hour follow-up (unpriced skeleton)

Send the follow-up within one business day, in the external client-facing voice (warm, plain, no emojis, no jargon). Reply on the running thread ("Prospect / TKCFO - Next Steps"). Skeleton:

  • Thanks and a specific callback. One line that references something concrete they said, so it reads personal.
  • The gap, named plainly. A short restatement of the specific problem we diagnosed, in their words.
  • The plan. What happens next and the outcome it produces (the free assessment or live-data demo, and what they will be able to see).
  • What we need from them. The one or two access items to start, stated with the money-movement boundary ("view-only: we can see transactions and statements, we cannot move money or approve anything").
  • Open items with owners and dates. Every question raised and not resolved on the call, each with "I will follow up on this by (date)."
  • Pricing pointer, not a number. If a tiered quote was promised, say it is coming by (date). Do not print a bespoke number here. Any figure that appears in a written quote comes from the pricing ladder (pricing-model/pricing.json, internal), never invented on the fly.
  • A clean next-step ask and a date. One action, one date.

Guardrails

Hard rules (read before every call):

  • No commitments in the room. No scope promise, no timeline promise, no discount, no "sure, we can do that" said live. Every commitment is Ricky's to approve and gets written down before it counts.
  • Live pricing is the narrow exception, not the default. A live on-ladder range may be given only for a simple single-entity SMB that meets every part of the ladder's simple-SMB gate: no payroll, no AP/AR, no multiple entities, no fund accounting, and revenue at or below the ladder's simple-SMB revenue ceiling (defined in pricing-model/pricing.json, internal). Even then it is an on-ladder range, confirmed in writing the same day, never a bespoke off-ladder number. Everything else (churches, nonprofits, multi-entity, anything with payroll or AP/AR or funds, or above the revenue ceiling) gets a tiered quote within one business day, no live number.
  • Never quote a bespoke number, live. The ban is off-ladder pricing invented in the moment. If it is not on the ladder, it is not said on the call.
  • Never let the client's crisis set our timeline. A departing bookkeeper, a board meeting next week, or a hard cutover date is a real constraint to plan around, not a reason to skip the full-stack inventory or promise a date we cannot hold. Run the complete diagnosis in the first call even under deadline pressure, then set a timeline we own.
  • Every promise made gets written into the handoff brief. If the deal is won, every commitment made on the call (verbatim) goes into the Sales to Service Handoff Brief. A promise that is not written down is a promise the delivery team cannot keep.
  • Diagnose before pricing. The assessment or the live-data demo is the pitch. Do not lead with a price.
  • Surface scope before signing, not after. The whole operational stack comes out in the first call. Scope that leaks in reactively after onboarding is the anti-pattern this runbook exists to prevent.

Voice and boundary rules also apply on every call and in every follow-up: Turnkey CFO is a bookkeeping firm (never the banned "fractional" phrase); tax and legal go to "your CPA or attorney"; client-facing email sends from a role address (accounting@turnkeycfo.com or the discovery lead's role mailbox), never a personal one; view-only means view-only, stated in writing; and no failure language ever reaches a prospect.

Escalation

Situation Action
Anything touching pricing, scope, or engagement terms Ricky, always. Never quote or promise solo.
Prospect pushes hard for a number the ladder does not support Hold; promise a tiered quote within one business day; bring the shape to Ricky.
Complexity surfaces that is above our lane (entity design, tax strategy) Redirect to "your CPA or attorney"; scope only the bookkeeping and reporting we deliver.
Crisis deadline the prospect wants us to hit by shortcutting diligence Flag to Ricky before committing; set a timeline we own, not the one the crisis dictates.
Prospect wants tax filing, audit, or legal work Redirect; do not sell what we do not deliver.
Deal marked won Discovery lead writes the Handoff Brief immediately (see Related SOPs); onboarding does not start until its path exists.
A "confirm with Ricky" open item (below) surfaces on a call Do not treat the unverified claim as precedent; confirm with Ricky before repeating it to anyone.
