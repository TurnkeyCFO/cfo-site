Purpose

Every client engagement is, underneath the bookkeeping, a pile of access: bank logins, payroll admin, card platforms, QBO, giving portals, lender sites. How we take that access in, where we keep it, and how we retire it is the difference between a firm a client can trust with their financial life and a liability waiting to happen. This SOP sets the one standard for all of it, so no secret ever lives somewhere it should not, and no departed person or ended engagement leaves a working key behind.

The governing principle is simple: prefer access grants over shared secrets, keep the few secrets we must hold in a vault built for it, and treat any secret that touches email or chat as already compromised. This SOP is referenced by the cutover handoff (capture and vault at first contact) and by the client welcome packet (how we ask for access), and its rotation rule is enforced at offboarding.

Owner and roles

Role Who Responsibility for credentials
Credential policy, vault ownership, rotation authority Ricky Owns the password manager, decides who holds what, executes token revocation and high-sensitivity rotations.
Intake execution and hygiene Accounting Manager Runs the secure intake, files secrets in the vault, catches and remediates a secret sent the wrong way.
Automation secret registration Whoever builds the automation Registers a machine credential in the workspace secrets store under the owning skill, never in code or chat.
Everyone All team Follows least privilege, never emails a secret, never borrows a login, reports an exposure the moment it is seen.

Cadence and triggers

  • At intake: every new access grant or credential is handled the moment it comes in, never left sitting in an inbox to be filed later.
  • On exposure: if a credential arrives by email, chat, or any non-secure channel, the redirect-and-rotate response runs the same day. There is no "it is probably fine."
  • On offboarding: every shared or stored credential the departing person or ended engagement could have known is rotated, not just reviewed, per the offboarding checklists.
  • Standing review: access is reviewed against the least-privilege table below whenever a role changes, and as part of the first-week access audit for every new client.

Who may hold what, by role

Access is granted by role and by need, not by seniority. This is the ceiling, not a default to hand out.

Role May hold
Ricky / admin The password manager itself, the workspace secrets store, token-revocation authority, and any credential the firm holds. Sole authority to rotate high-sensitivity secrets.
Accounting Manager Scoped client logins needed to run close and QC, filed in the vault; intake and rotation execution. Not the raw automation secrets store unless a specific task requires it.
Bookkeeper The platform access needed to do the books (QBO, the client's card and AP tools as scoped, Financial Cents). Not bank money-movement access, not the automation secrets store.
Admin / VA The tools needed for their tasks (calendar, CRM, comms). No client bank or payroll credentials.
Dialer rep Their own dialer and outreach tools only. No client financial credentials at all.

Guardrails

  • A secret in an inbox is a compromised secret. Redirect and rotate the same day, every time, no exceptions for "small" logins.
  • Access grants beat shared secrets. Always prefer inviting our role address or using an authorization flow over holding a password.
  • View-only means view-only, and we say so in writing. State the money-movement boundary to the client when requesting bank access.
  • One vault for human logins, one secrets store for automation, and nowhere else. Never a spreadsheet, a note, a chat, a dossier, or code.
  • Automation secrets name their owning skill. Each machine credential belongs to one automation and is documented as such.
  • Least privilege at intake and at every role change. Request the narrowest access that works.
  • Rotate on exit, do not just review. Removing a seat is not retiring a secret.
  • Never borrow a login or work around a missing one. Post the gap the day it is found and get proper access.
  • These pages never carry a secret. The lint gate and encryption boundary enforce it; do not attempt to smuggle one in with an allow marker.

Escalation

Situation Action
A client emails or messages a password Run the redirect-and-rotate response same day; if you cannot rotate it yourself, escalate to Ricky to coordinate the client.
A credential is exposed in a commit, a document, or a shared link Treat as compromised; escalate to Ricky immediately to rotate and purge; do not wait to confirm it was seen.
Unsure what access a role or person should hold Default to the least-privilege table above and confirm with Ricky before granting more.
A platform offers only broad access when narrow is needed Flag to Ricky; he decides whether to accept the broader grant with compensating controls.
A credential needed for close is missing or has stopped working Post the gap to Slack the day you find it; never borrow or work around it.
A departing person's credentials cannot be rotated same-day Escalate to Ricky immediately; treat it as a live security gap, not a to-do.
