Purpose

This manual trains a new admin or virtual assistant into the Turnkey admin seat from day one. The admin seat is the firm's rhythm keeper: you send the scheduled client touches on the monthly close calendar, you keep meetings booked and reminded, you keep the calendar and CRM clean, you file documents where they belong, and you support invoicing and social publishing. You do not answer questions about a client's books, and you never touch pricing, scope, or engagement terms. Those belong to other seats, and this manual shows you exactly where the lines are.

Turnkey CFO is a bookkeeping firm. Our client-facing language is bookkeeping, payroll, 1099, AP/AR, monthly close, and reporting. Tax or legal questions always route to "your CPA or attorney."

Read this once end to end, then work from the checklists. A new hire should be able to run this seat without asking what happens next.

Two things to know about the team around you:

  • You send on behalf of the firm, but you do not originate answers about any client's books. When a client replies to one of your sends with a question, you acknowledge and route it. That is the whole job of this seat done well.

The process

Welcome and the team around you

Welcome to Turnkey. The firm's promise to clients is simple: their financials always arrive on time, and nothing falls through the cracks. Your seat is where that promise becomes visible. Clients experience Turnkey mostly through the messages you send and the meetings you keep, so a clean, on-time, warm send from you IS the brand.

The work splits into six lanes, all covered below: monthly close sends, meeting scheduling, calendar and CRM hygiene, document collection and filing, invoicing support, and social publishing support. Every lane has a hard boundary: you run the process, other seats own the content and the decisions.

Your first week (access checklist)

Work through this with Ricky on day one and two. Post any access gap in Slack the day you find it. Never work around a missing login or borrow anyone's credentials.

Your 30/60 day ramp

Days 1 to 14: Learn the rhythm. Read the SOPs, shadow one full close cycle, and draft your first BD1 and BD3 sends. Everything client-facing you write goes to Ricky as a draft for review before it sends. Watch how the Accounting Manager decides what is ready.

Days 15 to 30: Run the sends with review. You own the BD1, BD3, and BD6 sends and the meeting reminders, with Ricky (or the Accounting Manager) reviewing each client-facing message before it goes out. Start owning the accounting@ triage and Drive filing fully.

Days 31 to 60: Independent on the routine. Scheduled sends, reminders, calendar, CRM updates, and filing run without pre-review. Anything new, unusual, or sensitive still gets a review pass. Social publishing approval follows the same arc: your first month is co-reviewed, then you publish independently with Ricky as backup.

Definition of success at 60 days: every scheduled send goes out on the right business day without being chased, every meeting has an invite and a 2-day reminder, the CRM matches reality, the Drive folders are clean, and Ricky only sees what genuinely needs him.

Communication for this role

Full rules live in Client Communication. The parts you use hourly:

  • Client-facing email sends from a role address (accounting@turnkeycfo.com or your @turnkeycfo.com address). Never a personal address.
  • External voice: warm, professional, plain-spoken. No emojis, no jargon. Lead with the answer.
  • Internal voice: direct and brief, bullets over prose, Slack channel over DM. Emojis are fine internally.
  • Onboarding and follow-up run as one named running thread per contact; reply on the existing thread, never a fresh email per touch.
  • Financial Cents Team Chat is internal; Client Chat is client-visible. Never mix them.
  • Broad client-facing memos take a "DRAFT for review" pass to Ricky before any external send.

Guardrails

  • You never answer questions about a client's books, numbers, or transactions. Acknowledge same day, route to the Accounting Manager.
  • Anything touching pricing, scope, engagement terms, or billing amounts goes to Ricky, always. Never negotiate, never promise, never hint ("I am sure that would be fine" counts as a promise).
  • Tax or legal questions get the standing line: "That is one for your CPA or attorney, and we are happy to get them anything they need from the books."
  • Never send client-facing email from a personal address. Role mailboxes only.
  • Never send an unreviewed delivery, and never send a delivery whose dashboard is not confirmed live. Hold and page Ricky instead.
  • No failure language ever reaches a client. If something is broken or late, the client hears a plain, calm status before the date.
  • Never send a team-wide reminder blast. Reminders are individualized: one person, only their items.
  • Never mix Financial Cents Team Chat and Client Chat, and never put internal commentary anywhere a client can see it.
  • Never work around a missing login or borrow credentials. Post the access gap to Slack the day you find it.
  • Never restructure Drive folders, CRM columns, or calendar structures unilaterally. Flag first.
  • Client-facing assets carry the client's own brand and palette, never Turnkey's swapped in.
  • Never write the phrase that pairs "fractional" with "CFO" in any outward copy. The top service tier is called Reporting & Advisory.

Escalation

The full ladder lives in Escalation and SLAs. Your seat's version:

Situation Your move
Client question about their books, timing, or a transaction Acknowledge same day, route to the Accounting Manager
Anything about pricing, scope, engagement terms, or an invoice amount Ricky, always. Holding acknowledgment only
Tax or legal question The CPA-or-attorney line, then flag to Ricky
Client frustrated, short, or gone quiet Flag to Ricky the same day with your read on why. Answer the routine part, never the emotion
Client sent nothing by BD3 Internal escalation the same day, then the warm nudge
BD6 package incomplete or dashboard not live Hold the send, page Ricky before the date
Reminder or send went to the wrong person or listed wrong items Stop everything in that batch, tell Ricky same day
Missing login or access gap Post it in Slack the day you find it
Unsure whether something needs Ricky Ask the Accounting Manager first. That question is what the middle rung is for

When you flag something to Ricky, use the NEEDS-RICKY pattern: post in Slack, include a screenshot or pasted copy of the client message, add one or two lines of context, say what you would do if it were your call, and mark urgency honestly. "Same day" means the client is upset or money is at risk; most things are "next sync."
