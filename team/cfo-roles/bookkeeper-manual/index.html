Purpose

Welcome to Turnkey CFO. This manual takes you from day one to full ownership of the bookkeeper seat: the person who gets every client's books roughly 90 percent of the way there each month. You own transaction entry, QBO categorization, reconciliation, first review of classifier output, and the open-items list that drives the close. Turnkey CFO is a bookkeeping firm: our services are bookkeeping, payroll, 1099, AP/AR, monthly close, and reporting. Tax and legal questions always route to the client's CPA or attorney, never answered by us.

Read this manual start to finish in your first two days, then work the checklists. Everything here links to a deeper SOP; this manual tells you which ones are yours and how they fit together in a real month.

Owner and roles

The bookkeeper seat sits at the base of a three-rung ladder. You draft the books; the Accounting Manager reviews and faces the client; Ricky holds pricing, scope, and the relationship.

You Bookkeeper Financial Cents and QBO (primary surfaces for this seat) Transaction entry, categorization, memos, reconciliation, classifier review, checkpoint lists
Accounting Manager (Beth) QC layer above you, client-facing liaison Slack plus the Outlook role address Reviews your work before any client sees it, sets your daily priorities, answers your routine questions

Nothing you produce goes to a client directly. You hand work up through the Accounting Manager, and client-facing email always sends from a role address such as accounting@turnkeycfo.com.

Cadence and triggers

  • Your month is anchored to the close calendar: BD1 request, BD3 checkpoint, BD6 delivery, BD10 lock, all counted in business days of the new month, closing the prior month's books.
  • BD1 through BD10 is your heaviest window every month. Plan availability around it; the first week of the month is a hard recurring deadline.
  • Daily during close: work the Financial Cents monthly project task lists, review classifier output, and update the open-items list per client.
  • Weekly: work the Ramp Needs Review queue for card clients, and prepare content for the individualized cardholder reminders.
  • Ongoing triggers: a new client onboarding hands you a cutover and a categorization-lane assignment; anything that looks wrong in the books triggers an immediate escalation, never a note for month-end.

The process

Welcome and the team around you

You are joining a small, systematized firm that wins on reliability: every client gets their prior month's books delivered on a fixed business-day rhythm, every month, without drama. Your seat is the engine of that rhythm.

Three things to internalize before anything else:

  1. The cadence is the product. Clients buy the certainty that BD6 delivery happens. Everything in your lane exists to protect that date.
  2. Memos are the coding signal. A card charge without a memo cannot be coded confidently. Chasing memo quality is real bookkeeping work, not admin.
  3. You never guess. Unknown transaction: flag it. Missing login: post the gap. Question touching money, scope, or tax: escalate. Guessing is the only unrecoverable mistake in this seat.

Working relationships:

  • The Accounting Manager is your first stop for every routine question and the person who reviews your work. Raise coding questions to her; she decides and directs.
  • Eldine sends the client-facing emails at each milestone. You feed her the content (what is missing, what is outstanding); she owns the send.
  • Ricky is not your first stop for routine questions, and that is by design. His attention is the firm's scarcest resource. When something genuinely needs him, escalate loudly with context; otherwise run it up one rung.
  • Handoffs to and from you happen inside Financial Cents Team Chat, tied to the client project, so context stays findable.

Tool deep-dives for this seat

QBO: the system of record. Every number that matters comes from QBO. Your working discipline: memo on every transaction, reconcile monthly to zero, match rather than add. Never restructure a chart of accounts, merge accounts, or add accounts without confirming with Ricky first. Never touch a closed period; if a change would land before the client's closed date, escalate instead. Never move money from any system: our access is read-and-record.

Financial Cents: your workflow home. A Workflow is a template; a Project is one client's instance for one period. Your task discipline:

Categorization lanes and classifier review. Three lanes exist; every client is explicitly assigned one at onboarding. Never assume automation coverage (confirm a client's lane with Ricky if it is not in their dossier).

  1. Manual (default). You categorize and memo every transaction in QBO yourself.
  2. Daily QBO classifier (pilot clients only). Automation proposes categorization; you review the output. Its write-back is hard-blocked until the client's closed-books date is configured, and an unset value silently routes everything to your manual review. Do not work around that gate.
  3. Ramp classifier (enrolled card clients). Ramp transactions are classified automatically and approved through a review card before anything writes back. It never mutates QBO directly.

Whatever the lane, you are the human who stands behind the books. Classifier output is a draft, not a decision: spot-check category choices against the client's coding rules, correct miscodes, and flag repeated misses so the rules improve. An automation error that ships is your miss, not the machine's.

Memo chasing and the reminder engines. Late card memos are the number one cause of a delayed close, so chasing them is core work, not overhead:

Per-client coding rules. Every client has quirks that generic bookkeeping gets wrong: fund and class tagging at churches, job or location tags, revenue-stream sub-accounts, related-party payments with specific reporting instructions, cash floats for event expenses, intercompany due-to and due-from activity between related entities. The client dossier is the source for these. Read it before coding a single transaction, and keep your own running notes current as you learn each book. Two standing traps: never collapse designated-fund classes into a catch-all, and never cross-code two clients that share an owner or a similar name.

Google Drive: the file store. File statements, reconciliation reports, and supporting documents into the client's numbered folder tree, named descriptively with client and date. Never leave a generic scanner filename, and never restructure folders unilaterally.

Communication and escalation for this seat

  • Your primary surfaces are Financial Cents and QBO. Client-facing email is not your lane: you prepare content, and the Accounting Manager or Eldine sends from a role address.
  • Raise routine coding and workflow questions to the Accounting Manager in Financial Cents Team Chat. Batch them where you can, and attach your recommended answer: "I think this is X because Y, confirm?" gets resolved in seconds.
  • Respond to internal messages within a couple of working hours during your workday.
  • If a client writes to you directly inside Client Chat about their books, answer factual, in-scope questions plainly and warmly. Anything touching pricing, scope, advice, or tax goes up the ladder with a same-day holding acknowledgment, never a guess.
  • No emojis, no jargon, and no internal shorthand anywhere a client can read. Warm, plain, professional.
  • Never write the phrase that pairs "fractional" with "CFO" anywhere a client could see it. We are a bookkeeping firm; the top tier is called Reporting & Advisory.

Mastery checklist

You own this seat when every box is true:

Guardrails

  • Never deliver or hand up books you would not stand behind. Materially incomplete means escalate first.
  • Never guess a category. Unknown transactions get flagged on the checkpoint list, not forced into a plausible account.
  • Match, never add, on suggested bank-feed transactions. Duplicates poison every downstream report.
  • Never touch a closed period. Nothing reopens after BD10; post-lock discoveries become next-cycle adjustments.
  • Never move money, approve a payment, or write anything to Bill.com or Expensify. Bank access is view-only; those platforms are read-only for us.
  • Never restructure a chart of accounts or add accounts without confirming with Ricky.
  • Never work around a missing login or borrow credentials. Post the gap the day you find it.
  • Never mix Financial Cents Team Chat and Client Chat. Internal commentary in a client-visible thread is unrecoverable.
  • Never answer a tax or legal question. The line is always "your CPA or attorney."
  • Never discuss pricing, scope, or engagement terms with a client. Not even "I am sure that would be fine." That is Ricky, always.
  • Never send a team-wide reminder blast. Reminders are individualized: one person, only their items.
  • Respect the automation gates. The classifier write-back block and the review cards exist on purpose; do not bypass them to save time.
