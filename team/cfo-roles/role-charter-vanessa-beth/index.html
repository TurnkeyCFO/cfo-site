Purpose

Turnkey hired two US client-facing people within a short window, and their mandates overlap. Without a written split, both seats wait on Ricky for direction and the two-in-a-box model breaks on day one. This charter resolves the overlap: who owns the client relationship day to day, who owns getting a new client live, who reviews what, and what both always escalate to Ricky.

This is a DRAFT-STANDARD, framed for Ricky to amend. The recommended default below is designed to match the seats each person already occupies in the live systems; where Ricky wants a different split, he changes it here and this becomes the standard. It is a RICKY DECISION.

Owner and roles

The recommended default draws a clean line: Vanessa owns the ongoing relationship, Beth owns the transition into it.

Seat Recommended default Primary surface
Vanessa: US Client Lead Owns client relationships day to day. Runs the review-tier client meetings. Is the BD6 QC reviewer. Approves the Financials-Ready pulls. This matches her existing Close Command role, where she runs reviews and approves the financials hand-off Close Command, the role mailbox, client meetings
Beth: Account Manager Owns the onboarding lane: new-client go-live execution end to end. Owns client-communication QC and escalation triage during and just after onboarding, then hands the ongoing relationship to the US Client Lead The role mailbox, the go-live checklist, Close Command

Why this split, not the reverse: the assessment shows Vanessa already runs reviews and approves Financials-Ready in Close Command, so making her the standing US Client Lead and BD6 QC reviewer formalizes what is already true. Beth's ministry background and client-facing QC strength fit the highest-variance, highest-touch moment in the lifecycle, the go-live, where a new client is either set up cleanly or inherits problems for months. Giving Beth the onboarding lane as a distinct seat mirrors the Blueprint's separate onboarding lane and keeps go-live from competing with steady-state client work for the same person's attention.

Both seats sit above the bookkeeper and below Ricky. Both escalate pricing, scope, and engagement terms to Ricky only, and neither ever negotiates those with a client.

Cadence and triggers

  • On a new signing: Beth owns the go-live. She drives the master client go-live checklist to completion and is the client's primary contact through onboarding.
  • At go-live completion: Beth runs the sales-to-service and onboarding hand-off to Vanessa, who becomes the ongoing US Client Lead for that client. The hand-off is a defined moment, not a fade.
  • Monthly close: Vanessa is the BD6 QC reviewer for her clients and approves the Financials-Ready pull; Beth can serve as QC reviewer for clients still in her onboarding window.
  • Review-tier meetings: Vanessa runs them from the standard agenda and report pack; board-tier and pastor-tier meetings stay with Ricky until he delegates them.
  • On a client-experience issue during or just after onboarding: Beth owns the triage; a steady-state client issue is Vanessa's.

The process

The division of labor, concretely

  • Client relationships, ongoing: Vanessa. She is who the client emails, who runs their review meeting, who knows their book.
  • Getting a client live: Beth. From signed letter to a client fully wired into every system and delivering its first clean close.
  • BD6 QC review: Vanessa for steady-state clients; Beth for clients still inside their onboarding window. Exactly one named reviewer signs off per client per cycle (see the QC Review SOP).
  • Financials-Ready approval: Vanessa approves the pull for her clients.
  • Client-communication QC and escalation triage: Beth during onboarding; Vanessa in steady state.
  • Pricing, scope, engagement terms: neither. Both flag to Ricky, acknowledge warmly to the client, and commit to nothing.

The client-assignment grid (to fill and amend)

The split above is by lifecycle stage; clients also need explicit assignment so each has one named owner. This grid is left for Ricky to fill and is amendable at any time. Every active client should have a US Client Lead named, and any client currently in go-live should have an onboarding owner named.

Until the grid is filled, the default holds: Vanessa is the standing US Client Lead, Beth owns any client currently onboarding. Ricky assigns and reassigns clients here; a reassignment is a note in this grid, not a renegotiation of the charter.

The onboarding-to-steady-state hand-off

The one place these two seats must not drop the client is the hand-off at go-live completion. Beth does not simply stop; she hands over.

Guardrails

  • Exactly one named US Client Lead per active client, and one named onboarding owner per client in go-live. No client with zero owners; no client with two people each assuming the other has it.
  • Neither seat negotiates or promises pricing, scope, or engagement terms. Both flag to Ricky and commit to nothing.
  • Neither seat gives tax or legal advice. The line is always the client's CPA or attorney.
  • The go-live-to-steady-state hand-off is a defined moment with an artifact, never a fade where the client quietly loses their onboarding contact.
  • This charter is Ricky's to amend. A teammate does not reassign clients or redraw the split on their own; changes are made here.
  • Client-facing email sends from a role address, never a personal one, from either seat.

Escalation

Situation What happens
Pricing, scope, or engagement-term question from a client Either seat: acknowledge warmly, flag to Ricky same day, promise a follow-up, commit to nothing
Unclear which seat owns a given client Check the assignment grid; if unfilled or ambiguous, Ricky assigns
A client in go-live has a steady-state-shaped issue, or vice versa The two seats decide who is closer and one owns it; if unresolved, Ricky decides
A frustrated or at-risk client Flag to Ricky same day with a read on why; the relationship save is Ricky's
Disagreement between the two seats on ownership or approach Ricky resolves it; do not let a client feel the seam
Ricky wants a different split than this default He amends this charter; the amended version becomes the standard
