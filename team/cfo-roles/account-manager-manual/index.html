Purpose

Welcome to Turnkey CFO. This manual is your training path for the Account Manager seat (the process SOPs call this role the Accounting Manager; same seat, same person). You are the quality layer between the bookkeeper and the client, the owner of the close calendar, and the person who makes sure every client feels looked after without Ricky having to touch every message.

Turnkey CFO is a bookkeeping firm. Our services are bookkeeping, payroll, 1099, AP/AR, monthly close, and reporting. We do not give tax or legal advice: those questions always route to "your CPA or attorney."

Read this manual start to finish in your first week, then work the checklists. It links out to the process SOPs rather than repeating them; the SOPs are the law, this manual is your map to them. By the end of your ramp you should be able to run a full close cycle, QC the bookkeeper's work, prepare a BD12 review packet, and know every client's engagement terms without asking.

Two structural notes worth internalizing early:

  • Your time zone and Hardik's create near 24-hour coverage on purpose. Hand work across the overlap instead of holding it overnight.
  • During your ramp, Ricky co-reviews your client-facing drafts before send. At full release that review step drops and you communicate with clients directly, with Ricky as backup.

Cadence and triggers

Your rhythm is built around the monthly close (business days of the new month, US-holiday aware):

  • BD1: monthly request goes out (you confirm each client's list is right before Eldine sends).
  • BD3: checkpoint. Books drafted, one consolidated open-items note per client. Card memos due from clients today. A client who has sent nothing gets escalated internally the same day.
  • BD6: delivery. Financials, refreshed dashboard, Open Items list. You QC before it ships.
  • BD10: true-up and lock. Nothing reopens after BD10.
  • BD12: client review meetings, spread across the BD11 to BD14 window.
  • Every Monday: Hardik and Ricky hold the weekly ops sync. Bring systemic patterns there, not ad hoc pings.
  • Every Friday: each client's open questions go out as one batched note with a three business day reply ask.
  • Daily: accounting@turnkeycfo.com triage pass (Thursday is historically the heaviest inbound day).
  • Same business day, always: acknowledge any client email, even if the full answer comes later.

Engagement letters: read every letter

Every client relationship is bounded by a signed engagement letter, and you are expected to know each one cold. The letter defines the services sold, the tier (Books / +Dashboard / +Advisory), the entities covered, and the start date. Scope questions you cannot answer from the letter are questions for Ricky, not guesses.

The per-client protocol

Repeat this protocol for every client you are assigned, and again for every new client that onboards. The output is a one-page working summary per client that lives in your head and in the client's dossier.

Onboarding coordination

Ricky sells and signs; you run the onboarding machine from there. The full checklist is the Client Onboarding SOP; your coordination job on top of it:

Watch-list monitoring

Keep a short personal watch list of clients showing risk signals, and review it every Monday before the ops sync. A client goes on the list when you see any of:

  • Repeated BD3 misses or chronic late memos.
  • A tone shift: shorter replies, formality where there was warmth, or silence from a previously chatty contact.
  • Meeting deferrals or no-shows on the standing review.
  • Books-level red flags recurring after being raised once.
  • Questions that circle pricing, "what are we paying for," or a competitor mention.

Working the list:

Where the watch list physically lives (a dossier note, a Slack thread, or a sheet) is your working choice (confirm with Ricky if he wants a standard location).

Upsell and scope-change flagging

You are the person most likely to hear expansion signals first, and the person least authorized to act on them alone. The play is always the same: acknowledge warmly, promise a follow-up, flag to Ricky.

Signals to flag:

  • A client mentions a second entity, a new location, a new revenue stream, or an acquisition.
  • A client asks for something outside their engagement letter (a budget build, a board pack, deeper reporting, AP takeover, payroll takeover).
  • A client's volume or complexity has clearly outgrown their tier.
  • A client asks what a different tier includes.

Day in the life and week in the month

A normal day:

Week one of the month (close week): the BD1/BD3/BD6 sequence dominates. Everything else yields. Protect BD6 by escalating BD3 silence the same day.

Week two: BD10 lock, BD12 meeting prep and meetings, premium-cadence reviews (second Wednesday). Meeting follow-ups turned into tracked items.

Weeks three and four: onboarding pushes, watch-list attention, dossier upkeep, payroll calendar verification for the coming month, Friday question batches, and clearing the ground so the next BD1 starts clean. Board-cadence reference dates (third Monday) land here.

Tool deep-dives for this role

Full platform detail is in Tools and Platforms; this is what matters most from your seat.

  • QBO: your review surface. Reads are open; you never write via workarounds, never touch a closed period, and never restructure a chart of accounts without Ricky. Match, never add.
  • Financial Cents: your management surface. Hardik lives here; assign and discuss work in Team Chat, keep Client Chat pristine and client-visible only. Status pills route follow-up: red "Waiting on Client" queues a chase, green means in flight.
  • Close Command: your radar. Check it every close-week morning; its checkboxes sync team-wide, so checking a box is a team communication.
  • Google Drive: your filing discipline. Everything that arrives by email or FC gets filed to the client's numbered folder with a descriptive name. FC is not the document of record.
  • accounting@ mailbox: your triage duty. The folder structure is the workflow: file fast, route bookkeeping items to Hardik's lane, route scope, pricing, or frustrated tone to the Ricky folder plus a same-day Slack ping.
  • Ramp / Bill.com / Expensify: your memo-discipline levers. Turnkey never moves money; Bill.com and Expensify are read-only; reminders are individualized per person, never a team blast.
  • CRM sheet: read freely for roster context; never hand-edit. Updates go to Ricky in Slack and get applied through the maintenance process.

Communication for this role

The full channel map and voice rules are in Client Communication. Your role-specific highlights:

  • You send from your @turnkeycfo.com role address, never a personal one.
  • Client-facing voice: warm, professional, plain-spoken. No emojis, no jargon, lead with the answer. Never the banned "fractional" phrase in anything outward-facing.
  • During ramp, every client-facing draft goes to Ricky for review before send. Broad client memos always take the DRAFT-for-review pass regardless of ramp stage.
  • Tax and legal questions get the redirect ("your CPA or attorney") plus a holding reply, then a flag to Ricky.
  • Advisory-shaped questions (margin, cash flow, budget strategy) get the holding reply and a same-day flag, never an improvised opinion.
  • Internally: direct, bullets over prose, channel over DM, explain why not just what.

Guardrails

  • Never negotiate or promise pricing, scope, or engagement terms. Not even "I'm sure that would be fine." Ricky, always.
  • Never give tax or legal advice. The line is "your CPA or attorney," every time.
  • Never deliver financials you would not stand behind. Materially incomplete months escalate before BD6, never ship quietly.
  • Never let failure language reach a client. Broken deliverable or dashboard: hold the send, page Ricky, fix it first.
  • Never mix Financial Cents Team Chat and Client Chat. Internal commentary in a client-visible thread is unrecoverable.
  • Never send client-facing email from a personal address. Role mailboxes only, no emojis, no banned "fractional" phrasing.
  • Never move money, approve payments past Ricky, or write to read-only platforms. View-only means view-only.
  • Never work around a missing login or borrow credentials. Post the gap in Slack the day you find it.
  • Never send a team-wide reminder blast when the item concerns one person. Reminders are individualized or they do not go out.
  • Never assume automation coverage for a client's categorization. Confirm the lane; unset gates force manual review by design.
  • Keep the client's own brand on their client-facing assets; never swap in Turnkey's palette.
  • PII (I-9s, W-4s, pay registers) travels through the named secure channel and lives in Drive only. Never in Slack, checklists, or open threads.

Escalation

You are the middle rung. Hardik raises to you; you decide what you can and pass up only what you cannot.

Situation Path

Anything touching pricing, scope, or engagement terms Ricky, always
Upsell or expansion signal from a client Acknowledge warmly, flag to Ricky same day, promise a follow-up, commit to nothing

Client frustrated, upset, or gone quiet Ricky, same day, with your read on why

Month materially incomplete at BD6 Escalate before shipping
Books-level red flags (unusual withdrawals, personal-looking spend, missing deposits, negative cash trends) Ricky, immediately, do not wait for a milestone
Hardik capacity or quality systemically off Ricky, with specifics: which clients, which patterns
Payroll transition date at risk Ricky before it blows, with the blocked items
You are unsure whether something needs Ricky That question is exactly what your seat is for on Hardik's items; for your own doubt, ask Ricky with a recommendation attached

When you flag: post in Slack, include the message or a screenshot plus one or two lines of context, say what you would do if it were your call, and mark urgency honestly. Same day means the client is upset or money is at risk; most things are next sync.
