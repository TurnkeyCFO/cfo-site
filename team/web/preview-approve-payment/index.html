Purpose

This SOP covers the middle of the Turnkey Web engagement: what happens after a draft site is built and before the client is live on their own domain. It explains how previews are delivered, how approval is captured before anything reaches a prospect or client, how the 50/50 build payment works, how the monthly care plan subscription starts after go-live, and how invoices actually get sent through Stripe with an internal approval gate.

The core idea: nothing goes to a prospect without a named human approval, and no money moves without Ricky's explicit sign-off. Follow the sequence and you cannot accidentally send an unreviewed site or an unapproved charge.

Owner and roles

Role Person Responsibility
Money and scope approver Ricky Sole approver on every invoice, subscription, refund, and any change to pricing or scope. Every Stripe action waits on his reaction in Slack.
Build approver Ricky (or the designated sales lead) Replies with the plain-text approval in the Slack thread before a preview link goes to any prospect.

Cadence and triggers

This SOP is trigger-driven, not scheduled. It fires when:

  • A draft build finishes and the build agent posts a "ready for review" card in the #tk-web Slack channel.
  • A prospect says yes and the engagement moves to billing setup.
  • A build completes and it is time to invoice the final half.
  • A site goes live and the 30-day clock starts for the care plan subscription.
  • The payment reconciler (which runs on a short polling cycle, roughly every 15 minutes) picks up an approval reaction or a paid event from Stripe.

The process

1. Preview delivery

Every draft site deploys to a preview URL at turnkeyweb.org/previews/<slug>/. The build agent posts a card to #tk-web in the format "Web build ready for review, , /100, N iterations, ".

2. Approval capture

There are two approval conventions, and they are not interchangeable:

  1. Build approval (human reply): Ricky or the designated sales lead replies in the Slack thread with an explicit plain-text approval, in the form " APPROVED". This is a typed reply, not an emoji reaction. Nothing goes to the prospect without it.
  2. Send approval (emoji card): automated follow-up sequences post an approval card that is approved with a white check mark reaction or rejected with an X. These cards expire after 48 hours with no reaction.

Whether these two tiers are a deliberate design or a pattern to standardize is an open question (confirm with Ricky). Until told otherwise, treat both as required in their own lane: builds need the typed "APPROVED" reply, automated sends need the emoji reaction.

3. The 50/50 build payment structure

The build setup fee splits 50/50: half before the build starts, half at completion and go-live. No dollar amounts appear in this SOP by design; pricing is set per engagement by Ricky.

  • Deposit (first half): invoiced before build work begins. Work does not start on a handshake.
  • Final (second half): invoiced when the build is complete and the client has approved the finished site, at or immediately before go-live.
  • Domain cost: the client's domain registration is a small pass-through cost, billed at cost, separate from the build fee.
  • One-off charges: extra work and change orders outside the agreed scope are billed as separate one-off invoices, never silently absorbed and never silently added to an existing invoice.

Some engagements include a time-boxed performance guarantee (new customers within a window, or money back) as a risk-reversal close. Whether a guarantee applies, and its terms, is Ricky's call per deal.

4. Stripe invoicing with the internal approval gate

All Turnkey Web money flows through one system: the turnkey-web-payments skill, backed by a single Stripe account. Every invoice and every subscription follows the same staged-then-approved pattern. No invoice reaches a client without Ricky's reaction.

Bookkeeping happens automatically: a Stripe-managed sync posts charges into the firm's QuickBooks file. Never enter these charges into QuickBooks by hand, and never bill a Turnkey Web project through the bookkeeping engagement flow. If a Web client is also a bookkeeping client, the two payment streams stay separate.

5. Subscription start after go-live

The monthly care plan is a custom per-client subscription that starts 30 days after go-live, not on the day the site launches. The 30-day gap is deliberate: the client gets a month with their new live site before the first recurring charge.

6. Go-live and domain handoff

Go-live moves the site off the preview path and onto the client's own address. Turnkey sites are hosted on Cloudflare Pages; each client gets their own repo and hosting project on a brand subdomain (like <client>.turnkeyweb.org) or on the client's own domain.

Guardrails

  • Nothing goes to a prospect or client without a named human approval in Slack. No rep sends a preview link on their own judgment.
  • No money moves without Ricky's reaction on an approval card. There is no auto-send path, no auto-charge path, and no exception for small amounts.
  • Never quote, discount, or negotiate pricing or scope yourself. That is Ricky, always.
  • Never send a broken, unfinished, or generic preview. If the build is not ready, hold and say so internally.
  • Never bill outside the system: no manual Stripe dashboard invoices, no personal payment links, no hand-entered QuickBooks charges for Web work.
  • Do not start build work before the deposit is invoiced and the engagement is confirmed.
  • Do not start the care plan subscription early or on the go-live day itself. It starts 30 days after go-live.
  • Keep Web billing and bookkeeping-engagement billing separate, even for the same client.
  • All client-facing email sends from a Turnkey role address, never a personal address, with no emojis and no jargon.
  • Tax or legal questions from a client route to "your CPA or attorney." Never answer them yourself.

Escalation

Situation What to do
Client pushes back on price, scope, or the 50/50 structure Escalate to Ricky before responding. Never negotiate solo.
Client disputes a charge or requests a refund Escalate to Ricky same day. Do not promise anything first.
An approval card expired before Ricky reacted Do not resend on your own. Flag it to Ricky in Slack; the item can be re-staged once he confirms.
A paid invoice is not showing as paid Ask for a reconcile check in the payments system before assuming the client did not pay.
Client goes quiet after the final invoice Flag to Ricky with a read on why. He decides the follow-up approach.
Prospect on a call turns out to need bookkeeping help Cross-brand referral opportunity. Flag it to Ricky; do not pitch bookkeeping terms yourself.
Anything feels off about a payment, a domain transfer, or an approval trail Stop and raise it in Slack before proceeding. A one-day pause beats a wrong charge.
