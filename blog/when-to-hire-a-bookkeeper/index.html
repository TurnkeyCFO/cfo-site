When to Hire a Bookkeeper: 7 Signs It's Time (and What It Costs)

By Ricky West · Founder, Turnkey CFO · June 3, 2026 · 9 min read

The honest answer to when to hire a bookkeeper isn't a revenue number on a chart — it's the moment your own time becomes the most expensive thing you're spending on the books. Most owners wait too long. They keep reconciling at 11pm on a Sunday because hiring feels like an admission they can't handle it, when the real story is that the business outgrew the spreadsheet two quarters ago. I've watched this play out across hundreds of small businesses, and the tipping point is almost never dramatic. It's a slow accumulation of small misses — a late 1099, a deduction left on the table, a cash crunch nobody saw coming — until the cost of doing it yourself quietly passes the cost of paying a pro.

This isn't a pitch to outsource everything tomorrow. It's a framework for reading your own numbers so you can tell the difference between "I should keep doing this" and "this is now losing me money." Below are the seven signs that DIY has flipped from frugal to expensive, followed by what each option actually costs in 2026.

Start with the math that reframes everything

Before any sign, run one calculation. Take what your time is worth to the business — not minimum wage, but what an hour of you selling, serving clients, or building costs when you're doing it instead. For most owners that's $75 to $200 an hour. Now multiply by the hours you lose to bookkeeping each month. Five hours a week, a common load once you're invoicing regularly, is about 21 hours a month. At even $100 an hour, that's $2,100 of your time poured into data entry and reconciliation.

A competent bookkeeper handling that same work runs a fraction of it. The question was never "can I afford a bookkeeper?" It's "can I afford to keep being one?" Once you've seen the number, the seven signs below stop feeling like luxuries and start looking like overdue corrections.

The 7 signs it's time to hire a bookkeeper

1. You can't answer "how did last month go?" without opening three tabs

If a lender, partner, or your own curiosity asks how the business did last month and you can't answer in under a minute, your books aren't a management tool anymore — they're a tax-time chore. A maintained set of books gives you a profit-and-loss statement on demand. When that answer requires reconstructing the month from bank statements, you've crossed from bookkeeping into archaeology, and archaeology is where expensive mistakes hide.

2. Your books are more than 60 days behind

Falling a couple of weeks behind is normal. Being two-plus months behind is a structural signal: the work has outgrown the time you've allotted it, and it will not catch itself up. Cleanup (often called catch-up bookkeeping) is its own paid project precisely because behind-books compound — every uncategorized transaction makes the next reconciliation harder. The longer you wait, the bigger the eventual bill. Owners who keep a tight handle on cash flow almost always have current books; the two move together.

3. You've added contractors or payroll

The moment money starts flowing out to people, the stakes change. Pay a contractor $600 or more in a year and you owe them a 1099-NEC by January 31 — to them and to the IRS, with no comfortable extension. Run payroll and you've added tax deposits, withholding, and filings on a schedule the IRS does not forgive. This is the single most common point where DIY breaks, because the penalties for getting it wrong are real money, not just a messy spreadsheet. If you've onboarded anyone this year and don't have their W-9 on file, that's your sign.

4. You're selling across state lines

After the Supreme Court's Wayfair-era shift, most states require you to register and collect sales tax once you pass roughly $100,000 in sales into that state (some still count 200 transactions). One strong out-of-state contract or a product that takes off online can create filing obligations in places you've never set foot. Multi-state sales tax is not a thing to learn on the fly — the registration, collection, and remittance calendar is exactly the kind of recurring compliance a bookkeeper is built to carry. When in doubt about your specific nexus footprint, talk to your CPA.

5. Tax season is a scramble, not a handoff

A well-run business hands its CPA clean books and a short list of questions. If your version of tax prep is a shoebox, a frantic export, and a prayer, you're paying twice: once in your own panic hours, and again in the higher fee your accountant charges to untangle raw data. Worse, the missed deductions in a messy year are invisible — you never see the deduction you forgot to record. That gap is often larger than a full year of bookkeeping fees.

6. You're raising money, getting a loan, or bringing on a partner

The IRS won't force most small businesses off cash-basis accounting until they average more than about $30 million in gross receipts, so this sign is rarely about regulation. It's about who's reading your numbers. The day a bank, an SBA lender, or an investor needs to see your financials, amateur books become a liability. Lenders want consistent, accrual-aware statements they can trust, and "my QuickBooks is mostly right" doesn't clear that bar. If a financing conversation is on your horizon, current professional books are part of the ticket.

7. The business has crossed roughly $150K–$250K in revenue

There's no magic number, but a pattern holds: somewhere between $150,000 and $250,000 in annual revenue, transaction volume and complexity outpace what an owner can maintain on the side without it costing more than it saves. Multiple revenue streams, a growing vendor list, the first hire, equipment purchases — they all land in this band. If you're industry-specific, the threshold can come sooner. Construction businesses juggling job costing and retainage, or cleaning companies managing recurring contracts and 1099 crews, hit bookkeeping complexity well before a simpler service business does.

What hiring a bookkeeper actually costs in 2026

Pricing is where most owners stall, usually because they imagine the most expensive option — a full-time hire — and stop there. In reality there are three tiers, and the right one depends entirely on your stage.

Freelance or part-time bookkeeper

A solo freelancer typically runs $30 to $60 an hour, or a few hundred dollars a month for a micro business with light volume. This is the lowest entry cost and works when your needs are simple and stable. The tradeoff is continuity — freelancers can be hard to replace, and you're often the backup if they're unavailable. Vet for someone who specializes in your accounting software and your kind of business.

Outsourced or virtual bookkeeping service

A monthly service is the most common fit for businesses in the signs above, generally $200 to $500 a month for a small business and $500 to $1,200+ as volume grows. You get a team rather than a single point of failure, defined deliverables, and software handled for you. If you're weighing this route, our breakdown of how virtual bookkeeping services work walks through what's included at each price point and what to ask before signing.

Full-time in-house bookkeeper

The Bureau of Labor Statistics puts the 2023 median wage for bookkeeping and accounting clerks near $47,440 a year (about $22.81 an hour), per its Occupational Outlook Handbook. Load in payroll taxes, benefits, software, and a workspace and a full-time hire realistically costs $55,000 to $70,000 all-in. That only makes sense at meaningful scale — typically well past $1 million in revenue with daily transaction volume. For nearly everyone reading this, it's overbuilt.

One Texas-specific note worth folding into your cost picture: the state has no income tax, but it does levy a franchise tax. The state raised the "no tax due" revenue threshold to $2.47 million for reports from 2024 onward, so most small businesses owe nothing — but the report is still due May 15, and missing it carries penalties. A bookkeeper keeps that on the calendar so a $0 obligation doesn't turn into a fine.

Match the spend to your stage

The trap is comparing the cost of hiring against zero. The real comparison is against what DIY is already costing you in time, missed deductions, late filings, and decisions made on stale numbers. Run the time-value math from the top of this article, check yourself against the seven signs, and pick the lightest tier that clears your needs. Most owners are best served starting with a freelancer or a virtual service and graduating up only when volume genuinely demands it. Frequently asked questions

Is it cheaper to hire a bookkeeper or do it myself?

It depends on what your time is worth. If bookkeeping costs you about five hours a week and your hour is worth $100+ to the business, a $300–$500/month service is usually cheaper than DIY once you count missed deductions and lost hours, not just the fee.

At what revenue should I hire a bookkeeper?

Most businesses benefit between $150,000 and $250,000 in annual revenue, or sooner once they add payroll, contractors, or multi-state sales. Complexity matters more than the raw number.

Do I need a bookkeeper if I already have a CPA?

Usually yes. A bookkeeper maintains your records year-round; a CPA uses them to file taxes and advise. Clean books from a bookkeeper typically lower your tax-prep bill and reduce errors.

What's the difference between a freelance bookkeeper and a virtual bookkeeping service?

A freelancer is one person you contract directly, often hourly. A virtual service is a team that delivers defined monthly financials and handles the software, trading single-person risk for reliability at a slightly higher cost.

How much does catch-up bookkeeping cost if I'm behind?

Cleanup is usually a separate project priced by how many months you're behind and how messy the records are. Costs rise the longer you wait, since each uncategorized month compounds the next.
