02 · The Short Version

You're growing fast. Wages are the only thing keeping the business at zero.

Short version: you're past the hard part. Deals are coming in, prices are up, Q1 2026 actually made money. The only reason you're not feeling it in your pockets is crew pay is eating almost everything.

This doesn't look like a failing detailing business. It looks like one that figured out how to charge real money — but has to pay almost all of it back out in wages. In 2025, for every $100 the business earned, $66 went right back out to the crew (with their payroll taxes). Healthy detailing shops run that number at $35 – $45 per $100.

A few smaller things also need cleaning up in the books. Some of your loan balances are off, some money got coded to the wrong places, and the P&L is showing a 96% profit margin that isn't real. Nothing scary — just enough that it's hard to see what's actually happening.

Close the wage gap and fix the book issues, and the business goes from losing about $4,400 in 2025 to earning around $27,500 a year on the same $100K of revenue you're already doing.

Eight things I saw in the books

The business is genuinely growing. Monthly revenue went from about $1,900/mo in early 2025 to $8,660/mo in Q1 2026. Average price per deal went from $227 to $456 — more than double.

Wages are the #1 issue. Crew pay plus payroll taxes ran 66% of revenue in 2025 and 68% in Q1 2026. Detailing businesses that actually make money run that at 35 – 45%.

The company truck ($20,361) was bought Feb 19, 2026, funded by a $20,000 loan in Kate's name through Santander.

Roughly 30 different people have been on payroll. The top 5 took more than half the wages. Everyone else averaged under $900 each. That's a lot of turnover.

A handful of bookkeeping slips to clean up — six savings transfers miscoded as loan payments ($6K off), $8,220 of unresolved opening-balance equity, a couple of maybe-duplicate deposits, and a ghost vehicle loan account from a weird typo.

The P&L says the business has a 96% profit margin, which is obviously wrong. Move the direct costs (wages, supplies, tools, laundry, uniforms) where they actually belong and the real 2025 margin is 9%.

Across 15 months you two have pulled about $13,700 out combined. David started getting a $230 biweekly paycheck in January 2026. Kate doesn't take anything. Average family take-home: $910 a month.

The family is carrying the business, not the other way around. $20,500+ in loans you personally guaranteed, another $6,400 on Kate's credit card, and all the day-to-day cash risk sits with Kate.

11 · Four Moves for the Next 60 Days

In order of what'll move the needle most.

These are ranked by impact, not effort. Number one is the big one — it's worth more than the other three put together.

Get wages down to 45% of revenue

Build a core crew of 3 – 4 people, stop rotating gig workers, use Connecteam to schedule and track job-level labor. Aim for 45% over the next two months, then work toward 40%.

Worth · +$18K/year at $100K

Capture customer names on every deal

You literally can't tell who your biggest customers are right now because most deposits don't have a name attached. Enforce it at the Acuity / invoicing step, not after.

Worth · actually knowing your book

Restart marketing at $500 – $800/month

The July 2025 ad spend is what triggered the Q3 breakout. Turn it back on, track by channel, measure cost per booked deal.

Worth · steady topline growth

Put the K&D loans in writing

Written promissory notes with a stated interest rate and a monthly payment. Amortization schedule in QuickBooks. Keeps taxes clean, keeps the family separate from the business.
